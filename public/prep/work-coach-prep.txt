Q: Tell me about a time you worked effectively as part of a team to achieve a shared goal. What was your contribution?
A: In my previous role we were under significant pressure during a period of high demand, with colleagues managing heavy workloads and a real risk that some customers would not receive support on time. My role was to manage my own caseload, but I could see the team needed more coordination to collectively meet our targets. I took the initiative to set up a brief daily ten-minute check-in so we could flag urgent cases early and redistribute work where needed. I created a simple shared tracker so everyone had visibility of caseload pressure points in real time. When a colleague was absent, I voluntarily covered a portion of their work to make sure customers were not left without contact. I also wrote up techniques that were working well for me in difficult customer conversations and shared them at our next team meeting. As a result the team maintained our contact rates throughout that period, customer satisfaction remained high, and my manager highlighted my contribution in my end-of-year review. I was then asked to mentor a new team member the following quarter.

Q: Describe a situation where you had to adapt how you communicated to help someone with limited understanding navigate a complex process.
A: I was supporting a customer who had very limited English and was struggling to navigate our digital services. They were at risk of missing a deadline that would have affected their benefit claim. I needed to explain a multi-step process in a way they could follow independently after our meeting. I stripped back all jargon and spoke in plain, short sentences. Rather than relying on the screen alone, I created a written step-by-step summary with numbered instructions. I used the teach-back method — asking the customer to repeat each step in their own words before we moved on. Where there were gaps in understanding I rephrased and used visual demonstrations on screen. I also gave them a printed copy to take home. The customer completed the process on time and their claim continued without interruption. Two weeks later they returned to tell me they had managed it independently the second time. My manager used that case as an example in a team session on inclusive communication.

Q: Tell me about a time you helped develop the confidence or skills of another person.
A: A new colleague joined the team during one of our busiest periods and was visibly overwhelmed by the pace and complexity of the caseload. I was asked informally to support their onboarding alongside my own responsibilities. I started by having an honest one-to-one with them to find out exactly where they felt least confident, rather than assuming. Based on that conversation I suggested we shadow each other in customer interactions — they observed me first, then I stayed present while they led, so they had a safety net without it feeling managed. After each interaction I gave specific, structured feedback — always starting with what went well before discussing what to improve, keeping it positive and practical. I also signposted them to the most useful guidance documents and scheduled a brief check-in at the end of each week to track progress. Within six weeks they were managing their caseload independently. They told me the shadowing approach had given them confidence to handle difficult situations without escalating. Their first performance review came in above the expected level for someone at that stage.

Q: What gives you energy at work?
A: I get genuine energy from the moment when someone moves forward after a conversation with me. There is something deeply satisfying about taking a situation that felt stuck — where a customer was uncertain or anxious — and leaving them with a clear plan and renewed confidence. The interactions that stay with me are when a customer comes back to say they got the job, or managed something they thought was beyond them. That sense of having changed something real for someone is what draws me to this kind of work. I also draw energy from being part of a team where people are committed to doing the right thing by customers. Even on difficult days, being around that shared purpose keeps me motivated.

Q: When do you feel most effective?
A: I feel most effective when I have listened properly. There is a real difference between hearing what someone tells you and understanding what is underneath it — their actual barriers, their fears, the things they have not said directly. When I take the time to reach that level of understanding, the support I offer is far more targeted and the outcomes are better. I also feel most effective when I can act decisively. I do not like things sitting unresolved — I would rather make a considered decision and adjust than wait indefinitely for perfect information. When I combine genuine understanding of what someone needs with the ability to act on it quickly, that is when I do my best work.

Q: Why do you want to be a Work Coach at DWP?
A: I want to work in a role where the impact of what I do each day is visible and real. The Work Coach role sits at the intersection of the things I care most about — practical support for people, coaching individuals through difficult transitions, and contributing to something that genuinely matters. I know the DWP serves around twenty million customers and that for many of them the Work Coach is the most significant professional relationship they have during a challenging period. I want to be part of that. I am also drawn to the breadth of the role — no two days are the same, and I thrive in environments that require adaptability, sound judgement, and the ability to build trust quickly with people from very different backgrounds. My experience in communication, caseload management, and supporting others makes me well suited to contribute from day one and grow within the role.
