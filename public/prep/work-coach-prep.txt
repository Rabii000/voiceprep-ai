Q: Tell me about a time you worked effectively as part of a team to achieve a shared goal. What was your contribution and what was the outcome?
A: At ASDA George I was part of a picking and fulfilment team operating under high-volume demand during a busy trading period. We were working against strict daily dispatch targets and there was real pressure on output without compromising accuracy, both of which had direct consequences for customer satisfaction. I could see that our picking routes were creating unnecessary crossover between team members, which was slowing us all down and generating small errors at the handover points. Rather than just managing my own output, I raised it with colleagues and we collectively mapped the bottlenecks together. I helped document our proposed revised routing structure and we presented it clearly to our supervisor, setting out the efficiency and accuracy benefits we expected. During the rollout I covered gaps when colleagues fell behind rather than leaving work to pile up, and when I identified discrepancies on the floor I flagged them immediately with a brief explanation and a recommended fix rather than just raising the problem. Our collaborative approach delivered a 20% reduction in processing time across the team. I personally maintained 100% order accuracy throughout and my supervisor specifically highlighted my contribution to team performance. That same instinct — to see beyond your own workload, flag early, and share what is working — is what I would bring to a Work Coach team, where colleagues are managing demanding and diverse caseloads simultaneously and the quality of shared support directly affects customers.

Q: Describe a situation where you had to adapt your communication style to help someone navigate something they found complex or unfamiliar.
A: In my role as an Editorial Assistant in academic publishing, I regularly communicated with authors and reviewers from a wide range of backgrounds, many of whom were navigating the peer review process for the first time and some of whom were not native English speakers. On one occasion I was responsible for communicating a return decision to an author whose revised submission had not fully addressed the outstanding reviewer concerns. The author had already found the process difficult and had indicated in earlier correspondence that the technical feedback was hard to interpret. My task was to explain the decision clearly in a way that was constructive rather than discouraging, and to give them the specific guidance they needed to address the remaining issues at the next attempt. I drafted a response that avoided jargon entirely and broke the feedback down into numbered points, each one tied directly to the relevant section of the reviewer's original comment so the author could cross-reference clearly. I acknowledged the effort they had already made and framed the remaining changes as achievable. I checked the draft with a senior editor before sending to make sure both the tone and the content were right. The author responded positively and said the breakdown had made the requirements far clearer than the reviewer comments alone. Their revised submission was accepted at the next review stage. Adapting communication to the person in front of you — especially when they are anxious, unfamiliar with a process, or come from a different background — is central to the Work Coach role. Many customers will be navigating Universal Credit, job search tools, or employment support for the first time. Getting that communication right is what makes the difference between someone leaving with confidence or leaving more confused than when they arrived.

Q: Tell me about a time you supported the development of someone else's skills or confidence.
A: During my cybersecurity internship I was part of a small team responsible for monitoring and responding to security alerts. When I joined, the team was handling alerts inconsistently, with no shared criteria for deciding what to prioritise. Response times varied significantly and some urgent issues were slower to reach the right person than they should have been. I identified this as a systemic problem rather than an individual one. I developed a structured triage framework with clear, documented criteria for categorising alerts by risk level, but rather than just circulating a document, I invested time in making sure colleagues could apply it confidently and understand the reasoning behind it. I ran a short briefing session using real examples from our alert log, inviting questions and adjusting the language until everyone was comfortable. I wanted them to understand the logic, not just follow a checklist, because that understanding was what would allow them to make sound judgements when an alert did not fit neatly into a category. Following the sessions, response times improved and the team was applying the criteria consistently and with noticeably more confidence. The number of unnecessary escalations fell and our supervisor noted the improvement in both speed and documentation quality. That experience showed me that supporting someone's development is not just about explaining a process — it is about understanding where their confidence is weak and building it deliberately, through clear explanation, real examples, and checking understanding before moving on. In a Work Coach role that same approach is at the heart of coaching customers towards employment — building skills and belief step by step rather than simply pointing them towards resources and expecting results.

Q: When do you feel most effective in your work?
A: I feel most effective when I give a situation the attention it genuinely deserves rather than processing it quickly and moving on. At Amazon that means taking the time to establish whether an inventory discrepancy is isolated or part of a wider pattern before acting — because the right response to each is different, and rushing leads to the wrong fix. In my editorial work it meant reading carefully enough to identify what an author had actually misunderstood, not just what they had failed to correct. At ASDA George it meant looking at workflow problems structurally rather than just working harder individually. I feel most effective when I have done enough groundwork to be fully present in the interaction itself — whether that is a customer conversation, a quality check, or a decision that needs to be clearly explained. When the preparation is right, the work that follows tends to go well.
