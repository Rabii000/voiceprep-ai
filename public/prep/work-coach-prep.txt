Q: Tell me about a time you worked effectively as part of a team to achieve a shared goal. What was your contribution?
A: In my case management role we were operating under significant pressure during a period of high caseload volume, with service level agreements at risk across the team. Policy changes had brought in a surge of new claimants and not all colleagues were yet fully trained on the updated guidance. My responsibility was to manage my own caseload within SLA, but I could see that without team coordination we would collectively breach targets and leave vulnerable customers without timely decisions. I took the initiative to set up a brief daily check-in so we could flag SLA-critical cases early and redistribute work based on capacity. I created a shared case tracker so everyone had real-time visibility of which cases were approaching deadline, and I volunteered to cover a portion of a colleague's caseload when they were absent so customers were not left waiting for decisions they were entitled to. I also drafted a short guidance note on the updated policy changes, walked through it with the team at our next meeting, and flagged the key decision points most likely to cause errors. As a result the team maintained compliance with our SLA targets throughout the period, quality review scores remained strong, and my manager highlighted my contribution at my next one-to-one. I was subsequently asked to support the onboarding of two new team members. The same skills — coordinating across a team, flagging risk early, and sharing knowledge of policy and process — are directly relevant to how I would operate as a Work Coach, where caseload accountability and collaborative working are equally central.

Q: Describe a situation where you had to adapt how you communicated to help someone with limited understanding navigate a complex process.
A: In my case management role I regularly handled inbound telephony from customers who were confused about decisions affecting their benefits or support entitlements. On one occasion I was speaking with a customer who had received a formal determination they did not understand and who had very limited experience navigating policy-based processes. They were anxious and at risk of missing a mandatory reconsideration deadline that would have ended their access to support. My task was to explain the decision clearly, walk them through the policy basis for it, and make sure they understood the specific steps they needed to take within the deadline. I stripped back all technical language and translated the relevant policy guidance into plain, step-by-step instructions. Rather than reading from the decision letter, I explained the outcome conversationally in language that reflected how the customer was speaking. I used the teach-back technique — asking them to repeat each step back to me before we moved on — so I could catch any gaps in their understanding and rephrase immediately. I also sent a written summary after the call that set out the three actions they needed to take, the deadlines, and who to contact if anything went wrong. The customer met their deadline, the reconsideration was submitted correctly, and no SLA breach occurred on the case. My manager cited the call as an example of good practice in our next quality review. This ability to translate complex policy into accessible language is something I would bring directly to the Work Coach role, where customers often need help navigating digital tools, Universal Credit processes, and job search requirements they find difficult.

Q: Tell me about a time you helped develop the confidence or skills of another person.
A: A new colleague joined our case management team during one of our busiest periods, at a point when we were working through a backlog created by a policy update that had significantly changed how we processed certain claim types. They were overwhelmed by the volume and the complexity of the guidance and were making cautious decisions that were taking longer than our SLA allowed. I was not their line manager, but I could see they needed practical support rather than just being pointed to the policy documentation. I started by having an honest one-to-one to find out exactly where they felt least confident — whether it was interpreting guidance, navigating the case management system, or handling difficult telephony contacts. Based on that conversation, I suggested we shadow each other on a selection of case types — they observed how I applied the relevant policy, then led on a similar case while I was available to advise. I gave structured feedback after each session: always starting with what they had handled well before identifying what to develop, so the feedback built confidence rather than undermining it. I also created a quick-reference guide to the most frequently misapplied policy sections, which I shared with the whole team. Within six weeks they were managing their own caseload within SLA independently. Their first quality review came in above the expected standard for a colleague at that stage. The experience reinforced for me that developing others is not just about explaining process — it is about understanding where someone's confidence is weak and building it deliberately. That is exactly the coaching approach the Work Coach role requires when helping customers who lack the skills or belief to move into work.

Q: What gives you energy at work?
A: I get real energy from the point in a conversation when something shifts — when a customer who came in anxious or confused leaves with a clear plan and the confidence to act on it. In case management that moment often comes after a complex telephony call where I have had to work through policy with someone who did not understand why a decision had been made, and by the end they feel informed and in control rather than overwhelmed. That change in someone's experience of a system that can feel impersonal is what I find genuinely motivating. I also draw energy from doing the job to a high standard — meeting SLAs, getting decisions right first time, handling difficult calls well. There is a quiet satisfaction in knowing that my caseload is managed accurately and that customers are not being let down by delays or errors. Working with a team that shares that commitment to quality and doing right by customers keeps me motivated even when volumes are high and the work is demanding.

Q: When do you feel most effective?
A: I feel most effective when I have properly understood what a customer needs before I act. In case management it is easy to process cases quickly without fully engaging with the person behind the file — but when I take the time to listen, ask the right questions, and identify what is really going on, the decisions I make are better and the conversations I have are more useful. I also feel most effective when I am operating confidently within policy and guidance, because that confidence means I can focus on the customer rather than second-guessing whether I have the right answer. The times I am most effective are when understanding of the person, knowledge of process, and the ability to act decisively come together in one interaction. That is the combination I would bring to the Work Coach role — genuine engagement with customers, grounded in sound knowledge of the support available to them.

Q: Why do you want to be a Work Coach at DWP?
A: My background in case management has given me a strong foundation in the systems and service standards that underpin DWP's work — managing caseloads within SLA, applying policy accurately, communicating complex decisions to customers across a wide range of circumstances, and working with colleagues to maintain quality under pressure. The Work Coach role is the natural next step from that experience, because it shifts the focus from processing cases to directly coaching individuals towards financial independence. That is the part of the work I find most meaningful — not just administering a process, but making a real difference to someone's trajectory. I understand what it means to be accountable for a caseload, to work with customers who are in difficult circumstances, and to maintain professional standards across telephony, digital, and face-to-face contact. I want to bring those transferable skills into a role where I can see the human outcome of the work — helping people find employment, build confidence, and take steps toward a more stable future. DWP's commitment to equality and inclusion also matters to me, and I want to be part of a team that takes that seriously.
