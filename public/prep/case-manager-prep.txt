Q: Describe a time you communicated clearly and sensitively in a difficult situation and influenced the outcome positively.
A: In my role as an Editorial Assistant in academic publishing, I was responsible for communicating a return decision to an author whose revised submission had not fully addressed the reviewer's outstanding concerns. This was a sensitive situation — the author had already found the review process difficult, had indicated in earlier correspondence that the technical feedback was hard to interpret, and English was not their first language. Simply notifying them of the return without proper explanation risked further discouraging someone who was already struggling with the process. My task was to explain the decision clearly, acknowledge the effort they had made, and give them the specific, actionable guidance they needed to address the remaining issues. I drafted a response that avoided jargon and broke the feedback into clear numbered points, each one directly cross-referenced to the relevant section of the reviewer's original comments so nothing was left ambiguous. I framed the outstanding changes as achievable and specific rather than vague, and I checked the draft with a senior editor before sending to make sure the tone was both constructive and accurate. The author responded positively, said the breakdown had made the requirements far clearer than the reviewer comments alone, and their revised submission was accepted at the next review stage. This directly mirrors the communication demands of the Case Manager role — explaining complex or unwelcome information clearly and sensitively to customers who may be stressed, unfamiliar with the system, or from diverse backgrounds — and getting it right so they leave the interaction understanding what they need to do and feeling fairly treated.

Q: Give an example of a time you had to explain complex information clearly in writing or over the telephone so that someone could take the right action.
A: In my cybersecurity internship I developed a structured triage framework for categorising and prioritising security alerts by risk level. The framework itself was straightforward once understood, but the challenge was that colleagues needed to apply it confidently in real time under pressure — which meant the documentation alone was not sufficient. I ran a series of short briefing sessions using real examples from our alert log to walk through how the criteria applied in practice. I deliberately stripped back the technical language and replaced it with plain, accessible terms that reflected how the team already spoke about the work. I used a question and check approach — explaining each category, showing a real example, then asking colleagues to apply the same reasoning to a second example before we moved on. Where someone was uncertain I rephrase using a different angle rather than just repeating the same explanation. I also created a one-page quick-reference card summarising the key decision points that colleagues could keep at their workstation. Following the sessions, the team was applying the criteria consistently and with confidence. Response times improved and unnecessary escalations fell. This ability to take something complex and make it accessible and actionable is directly relevant to the Case Manager role at Access to Work, where customers may need help understanding decisions, entitlements, and next steps that involve multiple systems and processes they are unfamiliar with.

Q: Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult decision with incomplete or conflicting information. How did you approach it and what was the result?
A: In my role as an Editorial Assistant I was managing a case where an author had submitted a revised manuscript following peer review. On the surface the revision appeared to meet the requirements — the author had responded to each reviewer comment — but when I reviewed the submission in detail I identified that one of the underlying methodological concerns flagged by the reviewer had not been substantively addressed. The author had acknowledged the issue in their response letter but the correction in the paper itself was insufficient. This placed me in a position where I needed to make a decision: progress the case, which risked it being rejected at a later stage, or recommend a return for further revision, which carried its own consequences for the author's timeline and confidence. I reviewed the reviewer comments and the revised submission carefully, mapping each specific concern against what had been changed and what remained unresolved. I did not make the decision unilaterally. I consulted a senior editor, presenting my analysis in writing — setting out what I had found, the specific sections in question, and why I believed the revision was insufficient rather than just different from what I had expected. I also considered the author's circumstances: they were not a native English speaker, had found the process challenging, and the communication about any return needed to be clear and specific enough to be genuinely actionable. The senior editor confirmed my analysis. The case was returned with targeted guidance, the author received clearer direction than in the initial review, and the revised submission was accepted at the following stage. I documented the full decision trail in the case file. This approach — gathering the right information, consulting where appropriate, considering the impact on the individual, and clearly recording and explaining the reasoning — is exactly what sound, accountable decision-making requires in a Case Manager role.

Q: Give an example of a time you managed a high workload to consistently deliver a high-quality service.
A: At Amazon I am responsible for managing high-volume fulfilment operations under strict service level agreements, including same-day delivery targets that leave no margin for errors to compound. To meet these demands consistently without sacrificing accuracy, I developed a personal prioritisation system — identifying the most time-sensitive orders at the start of each shift and sequencing my work around those first, while maintaining systematic checks throughout. I operate RFID scanners and automated inventory tools to update stock levels in real time, which requires discipline to use correctly every time rather than approximating under pressure. This structured approach has enabled me to maintain 99.8% inventory accuracy across my area and reduce fulfilment errors by 15%, while consistently meeting daily dispatch targets. At ASDA George I took a similar approach to managing quality under high volume. I worked collaboratively with colleagues to identify inefficiencies in our picking routes, and the changes we implemented contributed to a 20% reduction in processing time without any reduction in accuracy — I maintained 100% order accuracy throughout. I also conducted routine audits, identified discrepancies and reported them to supervisors with clear recommended corrective actions rather than simply flagging the problem and moving on. In my editorial role I managed multiple complex cases simultaneously across different stages of the review process, making prioritisation and escalation decisions that had real consequences for authors and publication timelines. Across all of these roles the principle has been consistent — quality is not about working faster, it is about applying the right level of attention to every task so that the outcome is accurate and the person depending on the service is not let down. I bring that same commitment to the Case Manager role, where decisions affect real people's access to support they may depend on.

Q: How do you stay motivated when the work is demanding or emotionally complex?
A: I stay grounded by keeping the person in mind rather than just the task. In my editorial work, when a case was taking longer than expected or required difficult conversations, reminding myself that the author on the other end was waiting for guidance that could affect their career kept the work purposeful. At Amazon, during high-pressure periods when targets were tight and errors carried consequences, I stayed focused by breaking the shift into smaller accountable segments — checking my accuracy at defined intervals rather than leaving the audit to the end. I also debrief with colleagues when something has been particularly challenging, because sharing that context briefly and moving forward is more effective than carrying it alone. I have a clear end-of-shift routine that helps me close the working day mentally and return the next day ready to do the work well. What sustains me across roles is the knowledge that the standard of my work has a direct impact on someone else's outcome — that is not pressure I find draining; it is the reason the work matters and it keeps me motivated to do it properly.

Q: What does delivering a high-quality service mean to you in practice?
A: Quality service to me means the person on the other end of the interaction leaves better informed, fairly treated, and clear about what happens next. It means accuracy — getting decisions right first time, based on the right information, with the reasoning clearly documented. At Amazon that means 99.8% inventory accuracy and SLAs met because every step is done correctly, not because corners were cut. At ASDA George it meant 100% order accuracy and a 20% improvement in processing time achieved through structural improvement rather than individual effort alone. In my editorial work it meant authors who found the process complex leaving with specific, actionable guidance rather than vague reassurance. Quality is also about consistency — every customer, regardless of how straightforward or complex their case, receives the same standard of care and the same honest, clear communication. In a Case Manager role that means every claimant can trust that their case is being handled thoroughly, that any decision affecting them is soundly based, and that if they contact the service they will be spoken to with respect and given information they can actually use.
